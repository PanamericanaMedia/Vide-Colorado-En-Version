Direct translation asks one question: what do these words mean in another language? Culturally resonant communication asks a different one: what does this audience need to hear, in the way they actually hear it? The gap between those two questions is where a lot of well-intentioned government and nonprofit communication quietly fails — technically accurate, and still missed.

A flyer translated word-for-word into Spanish can be grammatically perfect and still land flat, because it was written for one audience and handed to another without adjusting tone, references, or what's left unsaid. Bicultural communication starts earlier than translation: with who the message is really for, what they already believe, and what would actually move them. That's a strategy question, not a language question — and it's the reason Video Colorado works the way it does.

Culturally resonant communication consistently outperforms straight translation — because it starts with the audience, not the words.
